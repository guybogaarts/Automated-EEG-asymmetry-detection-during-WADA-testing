Calculation of relative QEEG measures

 (Het voorgaande deel van 2.4 hoort in Introductie en niet in Methode) Relative delta power was calculated by expressing FBE relative to the baseline EEG prior to injection by dividing it by the median value of the 5 minutes before injection (Tu 2015). The 3 midline electrodes Fz, Cz and Fz are evaluated as well to obtain a complete overview of all electrodes without excluding potential useful information beforehand. To exclude the injection side from the QEEG measure, the maximum of each left-right electrode pair is taken. This yields, including the 3 midline electrodes, a total of 11 relative FBE (rFBE). 

The goal of using QEEG measures as a monitoring tool is to mimic the visual evaluation of the clinical neurophysiologist, i.e. classifying each EEG epoch as either symmetric or asymmetric. Therefore, BSI and rFBE need to be combined in a single ‘BSI’ score (cBSI). Linear discriminant analysis  (LDA) is a method of optimizing the separation of two classes by finding a linear combination of a set of features (McLachlan 1992). For example, LDA has been proven to be useful for asymmetry detection of post-clamp changes during carotid endarterectomy (Mishra 2011). Here, we use LDA to find a combination of sBSI and rFBE, that best separates symmetric from asymmetric EEG epochs: 

cBSI=a*sBSI+b*rFBE Equation 2

The parameters a and b in equation 2 are weight factors calculated by LDA.
To avoid overfitting and subsequent over optimistic classification results, the patient based leave-one-out (LOO) cross validation scheme is used. With LOO, an LDA model is trained on all but one patient’s data and used to classify this left out patient’s data. This is then repeated for all patients.  
Finally a Kalman filter (KF) (Kalman 1960) is used for signal smoothing. A KF is especially useful for online tasks such as asymmetry detection during a Wada test because it does not introduce a computational delay in contrary to a central moving average filter as used in (Peters 2012). A KF is an algorithm that uses a series of noisy measurements (BSI scores) to produce estimates of an underlying system (actual hemispheric inactivation) state being more precise then when based on single measurements alone.
