Introduction


The WADA test ﻿﻿(Wada 1960) is a commonly used test to evaluate cerebral language and memory lateralisation in candidates for epilepsy surgery. The test consists of inactivation of a hemisphere through an injection of an anaesthetic, usually sodium amobarbital ﻿﻿(Buchtel 2002)(Magee 2012)(Patel 2011). The injection is into one of the internal carotid arteries through a percutaneously inserted catheter placed into the iliac artery ﻿﻿(Blume 1973)(Acharya 1997). During this inactivation of the ipsilateral hemisphere, memory and language tests are conducted with the awake patient to predict the possible risk of amnesia and language deficit after temporal lobe resection.
It is crucial for reliable assessment of memory and language lateralisation that the initiation, termination, and degree of hemispheric inactivation is known ﻿﻿(Peters 2012). The most common monitoring techniques used to assess hemispheric inactivation are a series of contralateral hand strength tests ﻿﻿(Wada 1960) and ipsilateral delta slowing in the electroencephalogram (EEG).  The start and end of a successful Wada test are respectively based on the moments at which contralateral hemiplegia occurs and contralateral hemiparesis begins to recover ﻿﻿(Wada 1960) ﻿﻿(Takayama 2004)(Mikati 2009)(Kim 2007). However, this technique has several limitations. To test arm strength, the patient has to cooperate which may distract the patient from memory and language tests ﻿﻿(Blume 1973). Furthermore, there is no standardized procedure regarding the execution and frequency of the hand strength test. An additional drawback of this test is that it does not provide continuous data and tends to overestimate the duration of hemispheric inactivation ﻿﻿(Simkins-Bullock 2000). Visual inspection of delta slowing in the EEG ﻿﻿(Blume 1973) ﻿﻿(Rausch 1984), on the other hand, does provide continuous data ﻿﻿(Peters 2012). However, due to a low inter-observer agreement, with a reported kappa score of 0.1 ﻿﻿(Abend 2011), this technique is also limited. The limitations of the aforementioned techniques have existed since the introduction of the Wada procedure and may have compromised their accuracy ﻿﻿(Acharya 1997). Nevertheless, the Wada test remains a commonly used pre-surgical evaluation tool ﻿﻿(Sharan 2011)(Roberts 2011)(Baxendale 2009).
