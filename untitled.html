Introduction

The WADA test ﻿(Wada 1960) is a commonly used test to evaluate language and memory lateralisation in candidates for epilepsy surgery. The test consists of an injection of an anaesthetic, usually sodium amobarbital or other drugs ﻿(Buchtel 2002)(Magee 2012)(Patel 2011), into one of the internal carotid arteries through a percutaneously inserted catheter placed into the iliac artery ﻿(Blume 1973)(Acharya 1997). During this inactivation, memory and language tests are conducted to predicts the possible risk of amnesia and language deficit after temporal lobe resection.
For reliable assessment of memory and language lateralisation, it is crucial that the initiation, termination, and depth of the hemispheric inactivation is known ﻿(Peters 2012). The most common monitoring techniques used to assess hemispheric inactivation are a series of contralateral hand strength testing ﻿(Wada 1960) and delta slowing in the EEG.  The start and end of a valid Wada test are respectively based on the times at which contralateral hemiplegia occurs and contralateral hemiparesis begins to recover ﻿(Wada 1960) ﻿(Takayama 2004)(Mikati 2009)(Kim 2007). However, this technique has several limitations. To test arm strength, the patient has to cooperate which distracts the patient from memory and language tests ﻿(Blume 1973). Furthermore, there is no standard regarding the execution and frequency of the hand strength test. An additional drawback of this test is that it not provide continuous data and tends to overestimate the duration of hemispheric inactivation ﻿(Simkins-Bullock 2000). Visual inspection of delta slowing in the EEG ﻿(Blume 1973) ﻿(Rausch 1984), on the other hand, does provide continues data ﻿(Peters 2012). Due to the low inter-observer agreement, with a reported kappa score of 0.1 ﻿(Abend 2011), this technique is also limited. The limitations of these techniques have existed since the introduction of the Wada procedure and may have compromised their accuracy ﻿(Acharya 1997). Nevertheless, the Wada test remains a commonly used pre-surgical evaluation tool ﻿(Sharan 2011)(Roberts 2011)(Baxendale 2009). Various quantitative EEG (QEEG) analysis techniques were recently introduced to eliminate subjectivity of visual EEG reviews ﻿(Peters 2012) ﻿(Tu 2015). The Brain symmetry Index (BSI) measure is specifically designed to measure inter-hemispheric differences Peters ﻿(Putten 2007)(Peters 2012). It is defined as the absolute value of the relative difference in the spectral density of the right and left hemispheres. However, BSI in its current form just like hand grip strength overestimates the duration of inactivation ﻿(Peters 2012). Furthermore, the presence of significant, but less evident, contralateral QEEG changes, even in the absence of significant cross-filling (observed by angiography) suggests that contralateral EEG changes are not only induced by direct perfusion of drugs into the contralateral hemisphere but also by a transient functional disconnection from the ipsilateral hemisphere ﻿(Tu 2015)(Ahern 1995)(Hong 2000)(Gotman 1992)(Douw 2009). Because of these contralateral EEG changes, the BSI score might be suboptimal for use as a monitoring tool during Wada tests. Tu et al. evaluated several QEEG measure of which delta power changes most closely mirrored that of the hemiplegia and amplitude-integrated EEG had the highest signal/noise ratio. Therefore, these quantitative values appear to be the best measures for decay of anaesthetic effects ﻿(Tu 2015). These QEEG measures were expressed relative to their median value of a baseline segment chosen prior to the first injection, they therefore contain additional information not captured by the BSI. First aim of this study is to combine two QEEG measures: the BSI and relative delta power. Another important factor in asymmetry detection during Wada tests is the choice of electrodes. It is common to exclude certain electrodes beforehand because they are more prone to for example eye-movement artefacts (Frontoparietal), or because they are thought to be irrelevant for quantifying inter-hemispheric differences (midline electrodes Fz, Cz and Pz) ﻿(Douw 2009). Wada tests are performed to evaluate language and memory lateralisation.  Language is mainly located in the inferior frontal gyrus (Wernicke’s area or Broca’s area) and memory located in the temporal lobe. Furthermore, contralateral EEG changes due to cross-filling occur mainly in the anterior cerebral artery region ﻿(Tu 2015). We therefore evaluate asymmetry detection performance of each of the 19 electrodes, including the midline electrodes. Finally, asymmetry detection using QEEG has so far not explicitly been evaluated in terms of how it performs compared to visual inspection of the EEG. The actual annotations provided by the clinical neurophysiologist online during the Wada tests were therefore used as the golden standard.  In this paper we aim at optimizing QEEG based asymmetry detection during Wada tests using the clinical neurophysiologists annotations as the golden standard.

Methods
Subjects and Wada tests
58 adult patients who underwent a Wada test between 2001and 2012 at Maastricht University Medical centre were included. Five registrations were excluded due to poor quality of the EEG recording. Patients’ age ranged from 19 to 61 (37 ± 12. Mean ±standard deviation). Patients were injected with 125 mg amobarbital and 42 patients with 150 mg. The 53 patients received a total of 85 injections, 22 patients received 1 injection, 30 patients received 2 and one patient 3 injections. After the amobarbital injection, a clinical neuropsychologist applies a series of memory and language tasks while a clinical neuropsychologist monitors and interprets the EEG. The memory and language tasks are stopped when the EEG is symmetric again, as indicated by the  neuropsychologist. These time points are recorded and used to annotate the EEG.

EEG acquisition
Ag/AgCl EEG electrodes were placed according to the international 10-20 electrode placement system. EEGs were recorded at a sampling rate of 256 per second using Schwarzer EEG29US  hardware and reviewed online with Brainlab software (OSG).

Calculation of the Brain Symmetry Index
EEGs were processed with in-house developed Matlab software ﻿(2012). The recordings are band-pass filtered between 0.5 and 25 Hz and subsequently divided in 10 second epochs on which subsequently analysis is performed. The BSI is defined as the absolute value of the average relative differences in spectral density of the left and right hemispheres. Our implementation differs slightly from the those published previously (Putten 2004)(Putten 2007)(Putten 2004). This is because it was more convenient to uses Matlab software previously written for EEG based seizure detection (Bogaarts 2014). The spatial BSI (sBSI) is calculated according to equation 1:

sBSI=  1/(11*8) ∑_(f=1)^11▒∑_(e=1)^8▒|(L_fe-R_fe)/(L_fe+R_fe )| equation 1

where e indicates the electrode pair and f  indicates the frequency bin.
First, the spectral density of eleven 2Hz bins ranging from 0 to 16 Hz (1 Hz overlap) is calculated for each EEG channel. Second, the absolute left minus right difference for each electrode pair at each 2 Hz bin are divided by their sum. Third, the average of these values over the 8 electrode pairs is calculated. Finally, the BSI is calculated as the average value over the 11 frequency bins. In addition to changes in spatial symmetry, diffuse change in contralateral EEG characteristics may arise as well due to cross-filling or by a transient functional disconnection from the ipsilateral hemisphere (Tu 2015) (Hong 2000)(Gotman 1992)(Douw 2009)(Ahern 1995).

Calculation of relative QEEG measures
A temporal BSI (tBSI) has been introduced which first quantify the relative hemispheric changes in the mean spectral characteristics of the right and left hemisphere (Putten 2007). Subsequently, square root of the product of both hemispheric changes is taken to obtain a measure for diffuse EEG changes. However, this method uses an offset correction factor which may not be accurately be identified due to high variability of real-world EEG signals (Putten 2007). Based on the findings of Tu et al., and the convenience of using our seizure detection software, we chose to use normalized filter bank energy (rFBE) in the delta frequency range (0.7-2.3Hz) (Tu 2015)(Bogaarts 2014)(Temko 2011). FBE is expressed relative to the baseline EEG prior to injection by dividing by the median value of the 5 minutes before injection (Tu 2015). The 3 central electrodes Fz, Cz and Fz are evaluated as well to obtain a complete overview of all electrodes without excluding potential useful information beforehand. To have and QEEG measure that does not depend injection side, the maximum of each left-right electrode pair is taken. This yields, including the 3 midline electrodes, a total of 11 relative FBE (rFBE). 

The goal of using QEEG measures as a monitoring tool is to mimic the visual evaluation of the clinical neuropsychologist, i.e. classifying each EEG epoch as either symmetric or asymmetric. Therefore, BSI and rFBE need to be combined into one final ‘BSI’ score (cBSI). Linear discriminant analysis  (LDA) is a method of finding a linear combination of a set of features (McLachlan 1992). For example, LDA has been proven to be useful for asymmetry detection of post-clamp changes during carotid endarterectomy (Mishra 2011). Here, we use LDA to find a combination of sBSI and rFBE, that best separates symmetric from asymmetric EEG epochs: 

cBSI=a*sBSI+b*rFBE Equation 2

The parameters a and b in equation 2 are weight factors calculated by LDA.
To avoid overfitting and subsequent over optimistic classification results, the patient based leave-one-out (LOO) cross validation scheme is used. With LOO, a LDA model is trained on all but one patient’s data and used to classify this left out patient’s data. This is then repeated for all patients.  
For this study a Kalman filter (KF) (Kalman 1960) was used for signal smoothing, a KF is especially useful for online tasks such as asymmetry detection during a Wada test because it does not introduce a computational delay in contrary to a central moving average filter as used in (Peters 2012). A KF is an algorithm that uses a series of noisy measurements (BSI scores) to produce estimates of an underlying system (actual hemispheric inactivation) state being more precise then when based on single measurements alone.

Results
BSI electrode subset evaluation
The BSI can be calculated using any combination of one or more left-right electrode pairs. With 8 left-right electrode pairs this yields a total of 255 combinations.  To investigate the importance of a single electrode pair, all possible combinations including or excluding a particular pair are compared. Figure 1 shows, for each left-right electrode pair, the overall AUC value distributions for the BSI calculated for all possible electrode pair combinations excluding (left boxplot) or including (right boxplot) a particular electrode pair. The blue line indicates the AUC value for the BSI based on a single electrode pair. The most prominent observation in figure 1 is the importance of the electrode pair F3-F4. When this electrode pair is not used in the BSI calculation, performance is significantly lower, irrespective of the other included electrode pairs (p<0.0001, Wilcoxon ranked sum test). Only 3 other electrode pair combinations yield a slightly higher performance (F3-F4 + P3-P4, or C3-C4, or C3-C4 and O1-O2).  However, when the AUC is calculated per patient, the Wilcoxon signed rank test indicates that these difference are not statistically significant (p = 0.52, p=0.57 and p=0.85). Difference between F3-F4 and the 7 other single electrode pairs, on the other hand, are statistically significant. Figure 2A shows the AUC values for the BSI calculated using the single left-right electrode pairs. Each boxplot indicates the distribution of the AUC values for the 53 patients for each left-right electrode pair. As can be seen in figure 2A, for the 53 patients, BSI based on electrode pair F3-F4 achieves a median AUC value of 0.96 (0.89 and 0.99, 25th and 75th percentile).

rFBE frequency and electrode evaluation
Similar to Figure 2A, shows the AUC value distributions for rFBE for each electrode pair, including the 3 midline electrodes. The best performance (median AUC = 0.96) is achieved by Cz and C3-C4 (p=0.42, Wilcoxon signed rank test). Also P3-P4 shows good performance (median AUC = 0.95) but is slightly worse compared to Cz (p=0.047, Wilcoxon signed rank test). Contrary to the BSI score which performs best using the frontal electrodes, rFBE performs best for the central, posterior and temporal regions.
LDA combination of BSI and rFBE
Based on the results shown in figure 2, BSI calculated using electrode pair F3-F4 and rFBE based on electrode C3-C4 are combined using LDA into the final cBSI score. Figure 3 shows a scatterplot of all rFBE-BSI points, red points illustrates epochs labeled as ‘symmetric’ and green points epochs labeled as ‘asymmetric’. The orientation of the green points relative to the red points indicate that a (linear) combination of rFBE and BSI better separates ‘symmetric’ from ‘asymmetric’ epochs. This is confirmed by the LDA model which assigns equal weights to rFBE and BSI. The blue line in figure 3 indicates the direction perpendicular to the linear combination of rFBE and BSI.     

Classification performance

Classification performance
Figure 4 summarizes classification performance for BSI, rFBE and cBSI. Figure 4A shows the ROC curves for the complete dataset. In figure 4B, AUC values are calculated for each of the 53 patient separately and their distributions displayed as boxplots. Both the higher area under the ROC curve and the per patient AUC values show that a significant improvement in classification can be accomplished by combining BSI and rFBE. The improvement in median AUC value from 0.96 to 0.98 (p = 0.0015, Wilcoxon signed rank test) equates to a relative improvement of 50%. The improvement from 0.91 to 0.95 in AUC calculated for the entire dataset is of similar magnitude (45%).
