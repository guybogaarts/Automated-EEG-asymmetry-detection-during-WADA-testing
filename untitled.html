Introduction


The WADA test (Wada 1960) is a commonly used test to evaluate language and memory lateralisation in candidates for epilepsy surgery. The test consists of inactivation of a hemisphere through an injection of an anaesthetic, usually sodium amobarbital(Buchtel 2002)(Magee 2012)(Patel 2011), into one of the internal carotid arteries through a percutaneously inserted catheter placed into the iliac artery (Blume 1973)(Acharya 1997). During this inactivation, memory and language tests are conducted to predict the possible risk of amnesia and language deficit after temporal lobe resection.
For reliable assessment of memory and language lateralisation, it is crucial that the initiation, termination, and depth of the hemispheric inactivation is known (Peters 2012). The most common monitoring techniques used to assess hemispheric inactivation are a series of contralateral hand strength testing (Wada 1960) and delta slowing in the EEG.  The start and end of a valid Wada test are respectively based on the times at which contralateral hemiplegia occurs and contralateral hemiparesis begins to recover (Wada 1960) (Takayama 2004)(Mikati 2009)(Kim 2007). However, this technique has several limitations. To test arm strength, the patient has to cooperate which distracts the patient from memory and language tests (Blume 1973). Furthermore, there is no standardardized procedure regarding the execution and frequency of the hand strength test. An additional drawback of this test is that it does not provide continuous data and tends to overestimate the duration of hemispheric inactivation (Simkins-Bullock 2000). Visual inspection of delta slowing in the EEG (Blume 1973) (Rausch 1984), on the other hand, does provide continuous data (Peters 2012). Due to the low inter-observer agreement, with a reported kappa score of 0.1 (Abend 2011), this technique is also limited. The limitations of these techniques have existed since the introduction of the Wada procedure and may have compromised their accuracy (Acharya 1997). Nevertheless, the Wada test remains a commonly used pre-surgical evaluation tool (Sharan 2011)(Roberts 2011)(Baxendale 2009).
Various quantitative EEG (QEEG) analysis techniques were recently introduced to eliminate subjectivity of visual EEG reviews (Peters 2012) (Tu 2015). The Brain symmetry Index (BSI) measure is specifically designed to measure inter-hemispheric differences (Putten 2007)(Peters 2012). It is defined as the absolute value of the relative difference in the spectral density of the right and left hemispheres. However, BSI in its current form just like hand grip strength overestimates the duration of inactivation (Peters 2012). Furthermore, the presence of significant, but less evident, contralateral QEEG changes, even in the absence of significant cross-filling (observed by angiography), suggests that contralateral EEG changes are not only induced by direct perfusion of drugs into the contralateral hemisphere but also by a transient functional disconnection from the ipsilateral hemisphere (Tu 2015)(Ahern 1995)(Hong 2000)(Gotman 1992)(Douw 2009). Because of these contralateral EEG changes, the BSI score might be suboptimal for use as a monitoring tool during Wada tests. Tu et al. evaluated several QEEG measure of which delta power changes most closely fitted the period of hemiplegia and amplitude-integrated EEG had the highest signal/noise ratio. Therefore, these quantitative values (aEEG is niet per definitie geschikt vanwege hoge SNR) appear to be the best measures for decay of anaesthetic effects (Tu 2015). These QEEG measures were expressed relative to their median value of a baseline segment chosen prior to the first injection, they therefore contain additional information not captured by the BSI. First aim of this study is to evaluate the performance of asymmetry detection using a combination of two QEEG measures: the BSI and relative delta power (waarom geen aEEG meer, gezien vorige alinea).
Another important factor in asymmetry detection during Wada tests is the choice of electrodes. It is common to exclude certain electrodes beforehand because they are more prone to e.g. eye-movement artefacts (Frontoparietal), or because they are thought to be irrelevant for quantifying inter-hemispheric differences (midline electrodes Fz, Cz and Pz) (Douw 2009). Since Wada tests are performed to evaluate language and memory lateralisation and  language is mainly located in the inferior frontal gyrus (Wernicke’s area or Broca’s area) and memory located in the temporal lobe, the temporal electrodes are likely to be of importance. Furthermore, contralateral EEG changes due to cross-filling occur mainly in the anterior cerebral artery region (Tu 2015). We therefore evaluate asymmetry detection performance of each of the 19 electrodes, including the midline electrodes.
Finally, asymmetry detection using QEEG has so far not explicitly been evaluated in terms of how it performs compared to visual inspection of the EEG. The actual annotations provided by the clinical neurophysiologist online during the Wada tests were therefore used as the gold standard.  In this paper we aim at optimizing QEEG based asymmetry detection during Wada tests using the clinical neurophysiologists annotations as the gold standard.
