Various quantitative EEG analysis techniques (QEEG) were recently introduced to eliminate subjectivity of visual EEG judgement (Peters 2012) (Tu 2015). The spatial Brain Symmetry Index (BSI) measure quantifies inter-hemispheric differences (Putten 2007)(Peters 2012). and is defined as the absolute value of the relative difference between the spectral density of the right and left hemisphere. However, BSI in its current form, just like hand grip strength testing, overestimates the duration of inactivation (Peters 2012). Furthermore, the presence of significant, but less evident, contralateral QEEG changes, even in the absence of significant cross-filling (observed by angiography), suggests that contralateral EEG changes are not only induced by direct perfusion of drugs into the contralateral hemisphere but possibly also by a transient functional disconnection from the inactivated ipsilateral hemisphere (Tu 2015)(Ahern 1995)(Hong 2000)(Gotman 1992)(Douw 2009). Because of possible contralateral EEG changes, the BSI score might be suboptimal for use as a monitoring tool during Wada tests. As a possible solution a temporal BSI (tBSI) has been introduced which quantifies the relative hemispheric changes in the mean spectral characteristics of the right versus left hemisphere (Putten 2007). However, this method uses an arbitrarily chosen offset correction factor which may not be accurately be identified due to high variability of real-world EEG signals (Putten 2007). Tu et al. (Tu 2015) showed that delta power changes most closely fitted the period of hemiplegia, and amplitude-integrated EEG had the highest signal/noise ratio. Therefore, delta power changes appears to be the best measure to reflect anaesthetic effects. Actual delta power is expressed relative to its median value of a baseline segment chosen prior to the first injection, it therefore contains additional temporal information not captured by the spatial BSI.
