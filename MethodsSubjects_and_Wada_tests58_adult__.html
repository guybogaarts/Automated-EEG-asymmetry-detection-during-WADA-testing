Methods

Subjects and Wada tests
58 adult patients who underwent a Wada test between 2001and 2012 at Maastricht University Medical centre were included. Five registrations were excluded due to poor quality of the EEG recording. Patients’ age ranged from 19 to 61 (37 ± 12. Mean ±standard deviation). Patients were injected with 125 mg amobarbital and 42 patients with 150 mg. The 53 patients received a total of 85 injections, 22 patients received 1 injection, 30 patients received 2 and one patient 3 injections. After the amobarbital injection, a clinical neuropsychologist applies a series of memory and language tasks while a clinical neuropsychologist monitors and interprets the EEG. The memory and language tasks are stopped when the EEG is symmetric again, as indicated by the  neuropsychologist. These time points are recorded and used to annotate the EEG.

EEG acquisition
Ag/AgCl EEG electrodes were placed according to the international 10-20 electrode placement system. EEGs were recorded at a sampling rate of 256 per second using Schwarzer EEG29US  hardware and reviewed online with Brainlab software (OSG).

Calculation of the Brain Symmetry Index
EEGs were processed with in-house developed Matlab software ﻿(2012). The recordings are band-pass filtered between 0.5 and 25 Hz and subsequently divided in 10 second epochs on which subsequently analysis is performed. The BSI is defined as the absolute value of the average relative differences in spectral density of the left and right hemispheres. Our implementation differs slightly from the those published previously ﻿(Putten 2004)(Putten 2007)(Putten 2004). This is because it was more convenient to uses Matlab software previously written for EEG based seizure detection ﻿(Bogaarts 2014). The spatial BSI (sBSI) is calculated according to equation 1:

sBSI=  1/(11*8) ∑_(f=1)^11▒∑_(e=1)^8▒|(L_fe-R_fe)/(L_fe+R_fe )| equation 1

where e indicates the electrode pair and f  indicates the frequency bin.
First, the spectral density of eleven 2Hz bins ranging from 0 to 16 Hz (1 Hz overlap) is calculated for each EEG channel. Second, the absolute left minus right difference for each electrode pair at each 2 Hz bin are divided by their sum. Third, the average of these values over the 8 electrode pairs is calculated. Finally, the BSI is calculated as the average value over the 11 frequency bins. In addition to changes in spatial symmetry, diffuse change in contralateral EEG characteristics may arise as well due to cross-filling or by a transient functional disconnection from the ipsilateral hemisphere ﻿(Tu 2015) ﻿(Hong 2000)(Gotman 1992)(Douw 2009)(Ahern 1995).

Calculation of relative QEEG measures
A temporal BSI (tBSI) has been introduced which first quantify the relative hemispheric changes in the mean spectral characteristics of the right and left hemisphere ﻿(Putten 2007). Subsequently, square root of the product of both hemispheric changes is taken to obtain a measure for diffuse EEG changes. However, this method uses an offset correction factor which may not be accurately be identified due to high variability of real-world EEG signals ﻿(Putten 2007). Based on the findings of Tu et al., and the convenience of using our seizure detection software, we chose to use normalized filter bank energy (rFBE) in the delta frequency range (0.7-2.3Hz) ﻿(Tu 2015)(Bogaarts 2014)(Temko 2011). FBE is expressed relative to the baseline EEG prior to injection by dividing by the median value of the 5 minutes before injection ﻿(Tu 2015). The 3 central electrodes Fz, Cz and Fz are evaluated as well to obtain a complete overview of all electrodes without excluding potential useful information beforehand. To have and QEEG measure that does not depend injection side, the maximum of each left-right electrode pair is taken. This yields, including the 3 midline electrodes, a total of 11 relative FBE (rFBE). 

The goal of using QEEG measures as a monitoring tool is to mimic the visual evaluation of the clinical neuropsychologist, i.e. classifying each EEG epoch as either symmetric or asymmetric. Therefore, BSI and rFBE need to be combined into one final ‘BSI’ score (cBSI). Linear discriminant analysis  (LDA) is a method of finding a linear combination of a set of features ﻿(McLachlan 1992). For example, LDA has been proven to be useful for asymmetry detection of post-clamp changes during carotid endarterectomy ﻿(Mishra 2011). Here, we use LDA to find a combination of sBSI and rFBE, that best separates symmetric from asymmetric EEG epochs: 

cBSI=a*sBSI+b*rFBE Equation 2

The parameters a and b in equation 2 are weight factors calculated by LDA.
To avoid overfitting and subsequent over optimistic classification results, the patient based leave-one-out (LOO) cross validation scheme is used. With LOO, a LDA model is trained on all but one patient’s data and used to classify this left out patient’s data. This is then repeated for all patients.  
For this study a Kalman filter (KF) ﻿(Kalman 1960) was used for signal smoothing, a KF is especially useful for online tasks such as asymmetry detection during a Wada test because it does not introduce a computational delay in contrary to a central moving average filter as used in ﻿(Peters 2012). A KF is an algorithm that uses a series of noisy measurements (BSI scores) to produce estimates of an underlying system (actual hemispheric inactivation) state being more precise then when based on single measurements alone.
