First aim of our study is to evaluate the performance of asymmetry detection using a combination of two QEEG measures namely: BSI and relative EEG delta power.
Another important factor in asymmetry detection during Wada tests is the choice of electrodes. It is common to exclude certain electrodes beforehand because they are more prone to e.g. eye-movement artefacts (Frontoparietal), or because they are thought to be irrelevant for quantifying inter-hemispheric differences (midline electrodes Fz, Cz and Pz) ﻿(Douw 2009). Wada tests evaluate language and memory lateralisation and language is mainly located in the inferior frontal gyrus (Wernicke’s or Broca’s area) and memory located in the temporal lobe, the temporal electrodes are likely to be of higher importance. Furthermore, contralateral EEG changes due to cross-filling occur mainly in the anterior cerebral artery region ﻿(Tu 2015). In our EEG 10-20 electrode setting we therefore evaluated asymmetry detection performance of each of the 19 electrodes, including the midline electrodes. Finally, asymmetry detection using QEEG has so far not explicitly been evaluated in terms of how it performs compared to visual EEG inspection. To evaluate this aspect, asymmetry annotated during online Wada testing by a clinical neurophysiologist were used as golden standard. In this paper we aim to optimise QEEG based asymmetry detection during Wada testing using the experienced clinical neurophysiologist's annotations as a golden standard.
