Regarding their relevance, our study evaluates the performance of asymmetry detection combining spatial BSI and relative EEG delta power.
Another important factor in asymmetry detection during Wada testing is the choice of EEG electrodes to include in the analysis. Wada tests evaluate language and memory lateralisation and language is mainly located in the inferior frontal gyrus (Wernicke’s or Broca’s area) and memory located in the temporal lobe. Therefore, the temporal electrodes are likely to be of higher importance. Furthermore, contralateral EEG changes due to cross-filling occur mainly in the anterior cerebral artery region ﻿(Tu 2015) and therefore the occipital electrodes are likely to be less relevant. It is common to exclude certain electrodes beforehand because they are more prone to e.g. eye-movement artefacts (Frontoparietal), or because they are regarded to be irrelevant for quantifying inter-hemispheric differences (midline electrodes Fz, Cz and Pz) ﻿(Douw 2009).  In our 10-20 EEG electrode setting it was possible to evaluate asymmetry detection performance of all 19 electrodes, including midline electrodes. Finally, asymmetry detection using QEEG has so far not explicitly been evaluated in terms of how it performs compared to visual EEG inspection by a clinical neurophysiologist. To evaluate this aspect, asymmetry annotations provided by a clinical neurophysiologist during online Wada testing were used as golden standard.
